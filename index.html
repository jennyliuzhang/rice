This Side of Rice

A Chinese-American's
(Ideal) Kitchen Table

By Jenny Zhang

Growing up in two cultures can mean choosing sides. The choice between soccer practice or Chinese school. English or Mandarin. Shaomai or chicken wings?

Why choose sides when you can have both? The only side I want to choose is a side of rice.

“In the States, you can buy Chinese food. In Beijing you can buy hamburger. It's very close. Now I feel the world become a big family, like a really big family.”
— Jet Li

America, the world, and all of humanity is about variety. If Jet Li says so, it must be true. We're family. Keep your mind, heart, and stomach open.

Ready to share a meal?

Sesame Balls

My favorite thing in the universe is the first bite of a sesame ball.

My second favorite thing is the second bite.

Doughy and crunchy.
The red bean paste inside is warm.

As a food group, I'd say it's a dessert snack, like ice cream or cookies. Perfect midnight munchies delicacy. And just like ice cream or cookies,
I always want more...

In the morning,

I like waking up
to eggs.

Some days I want them scrambled or on toast,
but some days I'm really craving steamed egg.

Steamed Egg

It's fluffy,

savory,

light,

and hot.

Add green onion and soy sauce.
If you're feeling spicy (and those days are the best), add chili paste.

The Chinese diet consists of many leafy greens, but my favorite is

Dou Miao

Also known as pea shoots, I grew up cooking and eating dou miao with garlic, oil, and salt.

Really similar to how you'd prepare rainbow chard or green beans:
super easy.

Every culture has that easy, staple veggie dish.

Dou Sha Bao

Craving dessert snacks again?

Red bean paste finds itself in many Chinese pastries
(not that I'm complaining).

Sweet. Chewy.

Warm.

Some mornings, I'll have a dou sha bao if I'm feeling celebratory.
Kind of like starting the day with a scone or cupcake. Or both.

Pork Shumai

They may not look it, but shumai are quite filling.

Shumai are small dumplings, usually
consisting of ground pork, ginger,
sesame oil, and crab roe.

They're just as tasty and satisfying as chicken wings  or  baby back ribs.

Always  savory

and so, so

good.

Chinese food can be many things. It can be savory, sweet, chewy, warm, delicious. One thing it is not, however, is a fad. Forget trendy, forget exotic. Chinese food came to the United States with my parents. Chinese food came by boat, by plane, through customs and not knowing the language. Through soup kitchens, through night shifts, through living out of the car. Through work visa, through homesickness, through phone calls across the world. Through naturalization, through just wanting what's best for the kids, through what it really means to be American. Through missing the sunsets on the Yellow River and rice with every meal, America has Chinese food because someone's mother missed the smell of garlic and sesame oil in her kitchen in Shenzhen.

A home, a family, a kitchen table of histories. Respectable, complex, and engrained in tradition just as much as homemade chocolate chip cookies or barbecued chicken wings. This is the meal I want to share with you. This side of rice.

We are so lucky to consume and experience so many sides of the world in America. Let us respect them. We're family. Keep our minds, hearts, and stomachs open.
